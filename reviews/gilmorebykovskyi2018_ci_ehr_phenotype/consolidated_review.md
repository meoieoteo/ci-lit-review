# Consolidated Review: gilmorebykovskyi2018_ci_ehr_phenotype

## Consolidation Status

Complete.

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Gilmore-Bykovskyi et al. show that cognitive-impairment signals in inpatient notes often appear as informal symptom language, not only as formal dementia diagnoses.

## Bottom Line

Use this paper for phenotype vocabulary and EHR text-source design, not prediction performance.

## What Cited For

- Importance of unstructured cognitive and behavioral symptom documentation.
- Need to include clinician language such as confusion, orientation, executive function, cognition, and agitation.
- Limitations of relying only on diagnostic codes, medications, or diagnostic terminology.

## What Not Cited For

- Predictive accuracy.
- Prospective dementia risk.
- External validation.
- Cardiology screening utility.

## Publication Status and Evidence Weight

Publication status: peer-reviewed. Evidence strength: moderate for terminology derivation; indirect for prediction.

## Evidence Strength

Moderate for phenotype-design rationale.

## Key Contributions

Manual derivation and reliability testing of cognitive/behavioral documentation categories in dementia patients' inpatient EHR text.

## Key Limitations

Known dementia cohort only, no controls, no predictive model, acute stroke/hip-fracture setting, and inability to distinguish delirium from dementia.

## Agreements

All reviewers agree the paper is useful for broadening note-feature vocabulary.

## Tensions

The paper is clinically relevant to cognitive impairment but not directly evaluative of any automated screening model.

## Label/Target/Timing

Claims-identified dementia; unstructured documentation from the last three hospital days.

## Validation/Transportability

Manual category reliability is good; transportability and prediction accuracy are untested.

## Bias/Downstream Action

Documentation reflects clinician observation and recognition; under-recognition may still leave some patients undocumented.

## Implications

A cognitive-risk model should not depend only on explicit dementia terms. It should consider symptom and behavior language from multiple disciplines.

## Claims to Carry Forward

- Unstructured notes contain broad cognitive/behavioral descriptors.
- Informal clinician language can be important for cognitive-impairment phenotyping.
- Multidisciplinary notes and flowsheets may carry relevant signals.

## Claims to Treat Cautiously

- Generalizability outside inpatient stroke/hip fracture.
- Ability of these terms to distinguish dementia from delirium.
- Predictive accuracy of derived terms.

## Open Questions

Which derived terms improve detection in outpatient or cardiology populations after accounting for delirium and acute illness?

## Recommended Next Action

Use as background phenotype-design evidence and pair with model-validation studies.
