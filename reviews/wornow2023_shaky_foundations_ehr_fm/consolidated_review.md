# Consolidated Review: wornow2023_shaky_foundations_ehr_fm

## Consolidation Status

Complete.

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Wornow et al. provide a peer-reviewed methodological warning that clinical foundation models are often evaluated on benchmarks that do not prove usefulness to health systems.

## Bottom Line

This is an important background citation for Section 8. It supports a cautious, evaluation-centered discussion of LLMs and EHR foundation models, not a dementia-specific empirical claim.

## What Cited For

- Distinguishing clinical language models from structured EHR foundation models.
- Showing that many model evaluations emphasize predictive accuracy while neglecting sample efficiency, deployment cost, multimodality, usability, fairness, and workflow capacity.
- Arguing that health-system usefulness requires clinically grounded evaluation metrics.

## What Not Cited For

- Dementia prediction performance.
- Cognitive impairment phenotyping.
- Cardiology-specific implementation.
- Systematic-review prevalence estimates.

## Publication Status and Evidence Weight

Publication status: peer-reviewed narrative review. Evidence strength: moderate for methodological framing; indirect for our clinical question.

## Evidence Strength

Moderate. The argument is strong and useful, but the review is narrative and not dementia-specific.

## Key Contributions

The paper gives a taxonomy of clinical foundation models and a practical framework for better evaluation.

## Key Limitations

No original validation, no dementia focus, no cardiology focus, and no systematic search.

## Agreements

All reviewers agree the paper should shape how we discuss foundation-model claims.

## Tensions

The paper is highly relevant to modeling strategy while offering little direct evidence about our patient population or outcome.

## Label/Target/Timing

Not applicable to a single label. Its relevance is in asking whether tasks and labels match clinical decisions.

## Validation/Transportability

The paper emphasizes transportability and evaluation gaps, especially for private EHR models.

## Bias/Downstream Action

The review explicitly flags miscalibration, subgroup fairness, overfitting, automation bias, and clinical utility.

## Implications

Our review should not present LLMs or foundation models as inherently superior. It should require evidence that the model improves the specific pre-encounter cognitive-risk workflow under realistic capacity and validation constraints.

## Claims to Carry Forward

- Evaluation should align with clinical action and health-system capacity.
- Larger clinical models need calibration, fairness, workflow, and usability evidence.
- Foundation-model advantages beyond accuracy are often asserted but under-tested.

## Claims to Treat Cautiously

- Any claim that a foundation model is clinically useful because it performs well on a generic benchmark.
- Any claim that few-shot or zero-shot performance removes the need for local validation.

## Open Questions

What evaluation metrics best match cognitive-risk screening before cardiology encounters, where clinical capacity and downstream action are constrained?

## Recommended Next Action

Use as an anchor citation in Section 8's modeling-strategy caveats and pair with task-specific dementia EHR studies.
