## One-Sentence Takeaway

Liu et al. shows that U.S. primary care clinicians and practices diagnose only a small fraction of expected MCI cases, making clinician/practice detection opportunity central to MCI labels.

## Bottom Line for This Literature Review

Direct Section 5 evidence for primary-care diagnosis opportunity and MCI undercoding.

## What This Paper Should Be Cited For

## What This Paper Should Not Be Cited For

True adjudicated MCI prevalence, EHR text methods, or cardiology-specific workflow.

## Publication Status and Evidence Weight

Peer-reviewed journal article. Strong direct relevance; expected-rate and claims-code limitations remain.

## Evidence Strength

`strong_direct_evidence`; `methodological_caution`

## Key Contributions

Average MCI detection rate of about 8%; clinician/practice-level evidence; geriatric clinicians detect more but still below expected.

## Key Limitations

MCI claims algorithm needs validation and expected rates are model-derived.

## Agreements Across Reviewers

All reviewers agree it is direct diagnosis-opportunity evidence and not an NLP paper.

## Tensions or Disagreements Across Reviewers

No major disagreement.

## Label, Target, and Timing Takeaways

MCI diagnosis is a documentation/care-recognition event; absence of MCI code is not a negative cognitive label.

## Validation and Transportability Takeaways

Claims method is reproducible; local use needs PCP attribution and validation.

## Bias and Downstream-Action Takeaways

Clinician/practice detection practices can determine whether MCI becomes observable.

## Implications for Study Design

Track pre- and post-index PCP continuity, primary care visits, geriatric/neurology contact, and cognitive testing.

## Claims to Carry Forward

Primary care MCI detection is very low in Medicare claims.

## Claims to Treat Cautiously

Exact rate estimates depend on HRS expected-rate modeling and claims definitions.

## Open Questions

Can local note text identify MCI evidence among patients without MCI codes?
