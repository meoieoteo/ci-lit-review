# Primary Review: wei2025_mci_adrd_phenotyping_ehr

## Review Status

primary_review_complete

## Review-Relevant Contribution

Wei et al. is direct evidence that combining clinical notes with structured ICD and medication features can phenotype MCI/ADRD more accurately than ICD or medication features alone. It is especially useful for label construction and EHR text signal, but it is a phenotyping/case-finding paper rather than future risk prediction.

## Publication Status and Evidence Weight

Peer-reviewed journal article. Strong direct relevance for MCI/ADRD EHR phenotyping and note signal. Evidence weight is limited for prospective deployment because labels were generated by single-neurologist chart review, uncertain cases were excluded, date ranges conflict in the article, and both sites are in Boston.

## Fit to Problem Statement

The paper supports the project's premise that unstructured notes contain cognitive-diagnosis information missed or poorly represented by structured codes. It helps with label construction and possible validation sample design. It does not define pre-cardiology risk scoring or downstream cardiology action.

## Clinical Target

Confirmed diagnosis of MCI or ADRD by manual chart review.

## Data and Cohort Relevance

Data come from MGH and BIDMC outpatient EHRs, age 50+, stratified by medication and ICD evidence. This is relevant to local EHR work but not cardiology-specific.

## EHR Text Relevance

High. Notes-only logistic regression substantially outperformed ICD-only and medication-only models. Top features included dementia-related terms and medications.

## Label or Outcome Relevance

High. The paper uses manual chart review to create yes/no MCI/ADRD labels and excludes uncertain cases. This is stronger than code-only labels but may bias toward clearer cases and lacks reported inter-rater reliability.

## Modeling Relevance

Moderate. The best model is relatively simple: LASSO logistic regression using TF-IDF note n-grams plus ICD and medication features. It supports strong simple baselines before LLMs or transformers.

## Validation and Utility Signals

The paper reports nested cross-validation, bootstrap confidence intervals, site train/test experiments, and error analysis. It does not report calibration, decision thresholds tied to action, or external validation outside Boston.

## Bias, Causality, and Downstream Action Issues

The model phenotypes documented MCI/ADRD diagnosis. It may still reflect documentation practices and specialty exposure, as false negatives arose when specialists in other areas mentioned MCI/ADRD sparsely. It does not address post-index diagnosis opportunity for future outcomes.

## Portability and Implementation Signals

Useful implementation details include ICD groups, medication list, note types, TF-IDF n-grams, and code/data availability. Portability concerns remain because MGH and BIDMC share a region, and performance differed by site.

## Key Extracted Claims

- Note-only model: AUROC 0.94 and AUPRC 0.94.
- ICD-only model: AUROC 0.54 and AUPRC 0.69.
- Combined ICD+medication+note model: AUROC 0.97 and AUPRC 0.97 in combined MGH+BIDMC setting.
- Best site experiment reached AUROC 0.98 and AUPRC 0.98.
- Estimated random-sample overall accuracy was 99.88%, using stratum-specific error rates and stratum prevalence.

## What This Paper Supports

- Clinical notes add major value for MCI/ADRD phenotyping.
- Code-only MCI/ADRD phenotypes can be weak.
- Simple interpretable TF-IDF plus logistic regression can be strong.
- Chart-review validation is feasible for cognitive phenotyping.
- Site-level testing matters.

## What This Paper Does Not Support

- Future dementia risk prediction.
- Cardiology-specific screening.
- ADRD subtype or severity identification.
- Generalization beyond Boston without validation.
- Fully adjudicated cognitive status with inter-rater reliability.
