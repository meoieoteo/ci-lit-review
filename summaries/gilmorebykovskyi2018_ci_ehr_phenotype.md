# Summary: gilmorebykovskyi2018_ci_ehr_phenotype

## Publication and Source Status

This is a peer-reviewed JAMIA article with full-text HTML downloaded from PubMed Central.

## Research Question

The study asks what unstructured EHR documentation describing symptoms of cognitive impairment looks like among hospitalized patients with dementia, with the goal of informing EHR-based cognitive-impairment phenotypes.

## Data and Cohort

The sample came from a retrospective cohort of Medicare beneficiaries discharged from an urban academic or urban community hospital to a sub-acute care facility between 2003 and 2008 after hip/pelvis/femur fracture or stroke. Dementia was identified using validated Medicare ICD-9 claims codes. The analyzed sample included 343 patients, with mean age 84.45 years; 74.1% were female.

## Methods

Trained clinician reviewers with nursing and neuropsychology backgrounds reviewed unstructured inpatient EHR data from the last three days of hospitalization. Sources included free text in flowsheets and provider notes such as progress notes, care plans, therapy notes, H&P notes, shift summaries, admission notes, and discharge summaries.

Reviewers extracted narrative statements about cognitive-impairment diagnoses and descriptions of cognitive or behavioral dysfunction. Statements were classified into 13 categories spanning cognitive and behavioral domains. A consensus validation process with geriatrics and neuropsychology experts refined the categories. A 10% random re-review assessed reliability.

## Results

Agreement for applying classification criteria was 85.8%, with Cohen kappa 0.82 and Gwet AC1 0.83. Of 343 records, 310 (90%) contained at least one narrative statement reflecting symptoms of cognitive impairment.

Across 2,444 narrative statements, 77% of applied categories represented cognitive dysfunction and 23% behavioral dysfunction. Common categories included global descriptors of confusion (28.9%), degree of orientation (20.9%), impaired executive function or reasoning (13.0%), global descriptors of cognition (7.3%), and agitation (15.2%). Most statements described a single symptom, while 26% described multiple symptoms.

The paper emphasizes that clinicians often used vague or general descriptors rather than formal diagnostic or assessment terminology. Fifty-six percent of statements reflected clinically derived descriptors, and 76% of cognitive-presentation descriptions were clinically derived.

## Limitations

This is an initial derivation study in patients already identified as having dementia and hospitalized for hip fracture or stroke. It did not include matched controls, did not evaluate predictive accuracy, and could not distinguish dementia from delirium or delirium superimposed on dementia. The data came from two hospital systems/EHRs and may not generalize.

## Relevance to Review

This paper supports the need for EHR-note phenotypes that include clinician language about confusion, orientation, executive function, agitation, and behavioral symptoms, not only diagnostic terms such as dementia or neurodegenerative disease. It is background/phenotype-design evidence, not model-validation evidence.
